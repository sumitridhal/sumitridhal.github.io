Treat the ground plane as a graph of a function `h(x, z)`. In practice `h` is almost always **layered stochastic noise**—the same fractal Brownian motion family described in [Noise in Creative Coding](/writing/noise-in-creative-coding)—because a single octave looks like a corrugated sheet, while summed octaves read as hills and valleys at several scales.

## Heightfield recipe

1. Pick world coordinates `p = (x, z)` (or UV if you are still prototyping in 2D).
2. Evaluate `h = fbm(p)` with a few octaves: each octave doubles the frequency and scales amplitude by a persistence factor (often near `0.5`).
3. Displace a dense mesh or tessellated plane along **up** by `h * amplitude`.

Normals follow from partial derivatives. On a grid you can finite-difference `h`; on the GPU you can bump `p` by small `ε` in `x` and `z` and reconstruct the tangent frame. Lighting only cares that those derivatives stay **continuous enough** that specular highlights do not crawl.

```glsl
// Conceptual fbm: replace snoise with your preferred primitive.
float terrainHeight(vec2 xz) {
  float h = 0.0;
  float a = 0.55;
  vec2 p = xz;
  for (int i = 0; i < 6; i++) {
    h += a * snoise(p);
    p *= 2.07;
    a *= 0.48;
  }
  return h;
}
```

## Domain warping for ridges

Straight FBM produces rolling hills. **Domain warp** pushes coordinates through another noise field before the height sample: `h = fbm(xz + warp * fbm2(xz))`. That shears crest lines into sharper silhouettes reminiscent of erosion, at the cost of harder-to-predict minima and self-intersection if you push `warp` too far. Keep warp magnitudes tied to the same length scale as your first octave so silhouettes stay legible.

## Two dimensions versus three

**2D noise over XZ** is cheap and matches how satellite heightmaps behave: each column is independent of altitude. **3D noise** sampled at `(x, y, z)` lets caves and overhangs exist if you later move to implicit surfaces or voxels, but costs more and tempts you to animate `y` and watch the whole solid swim unless you freeze the third coordinate per biome.

## Stability notes

- **Temporal**: if `h` feeds physics or LOD swaps, prefer deterministic seeds per chunk over `time` unless you intend morphing ground.
- **Tiling**: for infinite worlds, use tileable gradient noise, explicit periodic domains, or **blended chunks** with shared boundary constraints; naive repetition of the same texture shows a checkerboard of identical hills.

Once `h` is trustworthy, everything else—texture splatting by slope, river carving from downhill flow, or scattering instanced props by height bands—is a policy layer on top of the same scalar field.

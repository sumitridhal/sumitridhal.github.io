Editorial sites sometimes hide a hero photograph behind a **grid of blocks** that dissolve in irregular bands—denser toward the bottom, sparse at the top—so the image feels like it is resolving through noise rather than wiping with a single rectangle.

## Thresholds, not keyframes

Give every cell a number `t` in `[0, 1]`. Drive one scalar `progress` (from scroll, time, or a control). While `progress` is below `t`, the tile stays opaque; once `progress` passes `t`, fade the tile out. Bias `t` upward for rows near the bottom and add pseudo-random jitter so the silhouette stays jagged instead of a horizontal line.

That is the whole model: one timeline value, many independent comparisons. Scroll-linked demos usually map `progress` from `ScrollTrigger`; this note uses a **slider** so you can inspect the same mapping without fighting page scroll.

## DOM versus canvas

The embed above is pure DOM: an `<img>` and a CSS grid of small `div`s whose opacity follows `progress`. It is easy to reason about and avoids canvas readback rules for cross-origin textures. The tradeoff is cell count: a few hundred nodes is acceptable here; much denser grids belong on a single `<canvas>` or a GPU quad with a shader that encodes thresholds in a texture.

## Motion preferences

Readers who prefer reduced motion should not have to scrub past hundreds of tiles. The live preview respects `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` by forcing a fully revealed image and disabling the range control.
